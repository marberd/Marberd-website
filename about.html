Origin

It all began
when my
neighbor’s
house blew up.

I was young, but old enough to understand that buildings were not just walls, roofs, and windows. They were safety, memory, family, identity — and the fragile frame around everyday life.

That moment stayed with me. It became the first page of a much larger story.

I was eight years old.

Pieces of his wooden house landed in my grandmother’s yard, and while the adults saw debris, cleanup, and probably a very stressful neighborhood situation, I saw inventory.

There were boards. There was open space. There was a sheet nearby.

Naturally, I became a developer.

I gathered the wood, claimed a corner of the yard, and began assembling what I considered, at the time, a highly ambitious residential project. I leaned boards where they seemed to belong, moved pieces around with great authority, threw a sheet over the top for a roof, and sketched the whole thing with the confidence of someone who had absolutely no idea what she was doing.

My neighbor survived, thankfully. His house did not.

Long before I knew the language of plans, sections, elevations, structure, material reuse, or adaptive design, I was already doing the thing. I was studying what was available, imagining what it could become, testing ideas, drawing badly, building anyway, and convincing myself the final result was intentional.

That little playhouse was my first project. It had no permit, no budget, no consultant team, and almost certainly no structural logic. But it had a concept. It had personality. It had a roof, technically. And it had something I still chase in every project: the transformation of fragments into possibility.

Architecture, for me, did not begin as an abstract dream. It began in a yard, with salvaged wood, a sheet, and an eight-year-old girl who somehow understood that space could be made, claimed, imagined, and rebuilt.

That was the first page.

The rest is still being designed.
